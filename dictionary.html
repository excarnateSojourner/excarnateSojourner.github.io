acrid adjective (comparable) same as actual Acidic /ˈækɹɪd/
  1. Sharp and harsh, or bitter and not to the taste
  2. Causing heat and irritation
  3. Caustic; bitter; bitterly irritating
The cheapest way to clear logged woodland is to burn it, producing an acrid cloud of foul white smoke that, carried by the wind, can cover hundreds, or even thousands, of square miles.

—2013 June 29, "Unspontaneous combustion", in The Economist, volume 407, number 8842, page 29
becalm verb (transitive) /bijˈkɑ(l)m/ To lull into a false sense of security /bɪˈkɑm/
  1. To deprive (a ship) of wind, so that it cannot move
In the following two days, they made fast progress, strong easterly winds driving them down the Channel to where it opened out into the Atlantic; there, they were briefly becalmed.

—2011, Thomas Penn, Winter King, Penguin, published 2012, page 214
benevolent adjective (comparable) /bɛnəˈvowlənt/ Malevolent /bəˈnɛvələnt/
  1. Having a disposition to do good
  2. Altruistic, charitable, good, just and fair
This bill was not a gift from a benevolent legislature; it was a victory for the community which worked for its passage.

—1989 December 24, Kevin M. Cathcart, “Homophobia Never Innocent”, in Gay Community News, volume 17, number 24, page 5

  2. Held in veneration; revered
  3. Worthy of worship
Had I now had the sense to have gone back to Hull, and have gone home, I had been happy, and my father, as in our blessed Saviour’s parable, had even killed the fatted calf for me; for hearing the ship I went away in was cast away in Yarmouth Roads, it was a great while before he had any assurances that I was not drowned.

—1719, Daniel Defoe, Robinson Crusoe
